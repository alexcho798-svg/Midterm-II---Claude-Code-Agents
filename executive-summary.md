# Executive Summary: Key Findings for Financial Literacy Programme Design

Based on a survey of 500 young professionals across Latin America (`data/latam_finanzas_clean.csv`). Prepared for nonprofit leadership designing financial literacy programming.

**Finding 1: Income Differences Across Latin American Countries**
Median monthly income ranges from $798 in Argentina to $1,458 in Brazil — Brazilian respondents earn nearly double their Argentine counterparts. Brazil also shows the widest spread in earnings among its own respondents, meaning income there is far less consistent than in a country like Peru, where most people cluster closer to the median. This means a single "one-size-fits-all" curriculum, priced or benchmarked around one country's income level, will feel irrelevant or unaffordable elsewhere. **Recommendation:** localize budgeting benchmarks and examples by country rather than using a single regional average, and in Brazil specifically, offer tiered content given how unevenly income is distributed there.

**Finding 2: The Relationship Between Age and Savings Behaviour**
Savings rate nearly triples with age: 18–22 year-olds save just 5.7% of their income ($61/month on average), while 29–32 year-olds save 15.5% ($154/month). This group of 18–25 year-olds also makes up more than half the sample (285 of 500 respondents), meaning the segment with the weakest savings habit is also the largest audience available to reach. Since financial habits compound over time, reaching people before this gap widens further has outsized long-term value. **Recommendation:** prioritize an early "start small" savings-habit module for the 18–25 segment — automated micro-savings and habit-building content — rather than waiting until income (and expenses) grow.

**Finding 3: Where the Biggest Expense Categories Are**
Housing (28.5% of income) and food (23.8%) together consume more than half of the average respondent's monthly income, dwarfing transport, entertainment, and education, which each take roughly 8–10%. Healthcare is the smallest line item at just 4.9%. Because housing and food are largely non-discretionary, generic "spend less" advice won't move the needle much — the real leverage is in how people manage these two specific costs. **Recommendation:** build the core curriculum around practical housing-cost strategies (e.g., roommate-splitting, rent-to-income ratio guidelines) and smart grocery/meal planning, since these two categories hold the largest realistic savings potential.

**Finding 4: How Credit Card Holders Differ From Non-Holders**
Credit card holders earn only 1.5% more than non-holders on average — a negligible difference — yet they spend 16.1% more on food and 17.2% more on entertainment. Despite this extra spending, they still manage to save 6.7% more, suggesting the card isn't simply draining their finances, but it is clearly changing spending behaviour independent of income. This tells us the card itself, not income level, is the behavioural trigger worth addressing. **Recommendation:** for credit-card-holding participants, prioritize a spending-awareness module ("know your leakage" tracking for food and entertainment) rather than income-focused content, since income isn't what differentiates this group.

**Finding 5: The Relationship Between AI Tool Usage and Financial Satisfaction**
Financial satisfaction rises with AI tool usage: light users (0–3 hrs/week, 150 people) average 2.11 out of 5, moderate users (4–10 hrs/week, 335 people) average 2.60, and heavy users (11+ hrs/week, only 15 people) average 3.53 — a moderate positive relationship overall (r=0.57). However, that heaviest-usage group also has by far the highest income ($1,895 vs. $776 for light users) and is a very small sample, so this likely reflects who has access to these tools rather than proof that AI tools alone drive satisfaction. **Recommendation:** treat this as an access gap, not a proven cause-and-effect — introduce basic AI-powered budgeting tool literacy specifically for lower-income participants, who are currently the least likely to be using these tools.

**Finding 6: Housing Burden Differences by Country**
Housing costs as a share of income range from 24.6% in Peru to 34.1% in Argentina, with Argentina and Chile both exceeding the commonly-cited 30% affordability threshold. In these two countries, housing pressure is a structural constraint — no amount of budgeting skill can fully offset a fixed cost that's already unaffordable by international standards. **Recommendation:** for Argentina and Chile specifically, add a dedicated housing-strategy module (shared housing, rent negotiation, subsidy navigation) rather than folding housing into general budgeting advice as if it were a discretionary expense.
